## Goal
Identify periods and behaviours that deviate meaningfully from the typical
patterns established in Section 2. The aim is not to flag every statistical
outlier, but to surface actionable signals that a trading desk, risk manager,
or compliance officer would genuinely act on.

### Limitation — Wash Trade Screen

A formal wash trade screen requires multiple sessions of data to
establish a repeated pattern of self-crossing with flat net exposure.
On a single 60-minute session with ~8 events per second, coincidental
opposite-side matches within a 1-second window are statistically
expected and do not constitute evidence of manipulation.

This screen is therefore presented as a **structural demonstration**
of how a wash trade surveillance framework would be implemented in
production, rather than a definitive compliance finding. Any flags
produced should be treated as candidates for multi-session follow-up
investigation, not immediate escalation.

In production, this screen would be run across 30+ days of data with
three additional filters applied:
- Net position must remain flat after the cross
- Price of buy and sell must be within 1 tick of each other
- Pattern must repeat across multiple sessions

## Approach
Rather than applying generic anomaly detection, I anchor each signal directly
to leads identified during the basic analytics in Section 2:

- **Lead 1 — Minute 39:** Flagged in Section 2.4 as the session peak
  cancel-to-trade ratio (0.76x). Section 3 investigates who was cancelling,
  on which instrument, and whether it constitutes abnormal behaviour.
- **Lead 2 — Order ID Collision:** Identified in Section 2.3b. 27 orders
  share IDs across different instruments and sides — a data integrity anomaly
  formally catalogued here.
- **Lead 3 — NAS100 Block Trades:** Identified in Section 2.5. All top 10
  largest notional trades are NAS100, with the single largest at $26.85M.
  Section 3 investigates whether these are isolated or clustered in time.

This evidence-led approach ensures every signal has a pre-existing empirical
basis rather than being generated by arbitrary threshold selection.

## Wash Trade Screen — Structural Demonstration (Updated idea)

### Important Caveat
A formal wash trade investigation requires multiple sessions of data
to establish repeated crossing patterns with confirmed flat net exposure.
On a single 60-minute session with approximately 8 events per second,
coincidental opposite-side matches are statistically expected and do not
constitute evidence of manipulation.

This screen is presented as a **structural demonstration** of how a
wash trade surveillance framework would be implemented in production.
Any flags produced are candidates for multi-session follow-up
investigation — not immediate compliance escalation.

### Tightened Criteria (Option A)
To minimise false positives within a single session, three conditions
must all be satisfied simultaneously:

| Condition | Value | Justification |
|---|---|---|
| Same `client_tag` | Exact match | Beneficial owner boundary |
| Same `sym` | Exact match | Same instrument required |
| Same `ts_seconds` | Exact integer second | Eliminates coincidental near-matches |
| Same `price` | Exact match | Identical price = no market risk transfer |
| Same `quantity` | Exact match | Identical size = no economic purpose |
| Opposite `side` | buy vs sell | Required for a cross |

Requiring exact price **and** exact quantity **and** same second reduces
the probability of a coincidental flag to near zero — at that level of
specificity, a match is almost certainly a genuine self-cross.

### In Production This Screen Would Also Apply
- Net position flat check after each cross
- Pattern repetition across 30+ days of data
- Price within 1 tick (not just exact) to catch near-crosses
- Notional threshold filter to ignore trivially small crosses

## Wash Trade Screen (old idea)

### Definition
A wash trade occurs when a buyer and seller of the same instrument are
controlled by the same beneficial owner — creating artificial volume with
no genuine change in economic exposure. This is a form of market manipulation
prohibited by the FCA, SEC, and all major financial regulators.

### Why This Matters
- **Legal boundary (`client_tag`):** Regulators define wash trading at the
  beneficial owner level. If the same client uses Strategy X to buy and
  Strategy Y to sell simultaneously, it constitutes manipulation regardless
  of whether the strategies are technically separate.
- **Technical boundary (`endtag`):** If a single strategy crosses itself,
  it indicates a broken algorithm. If two strategies under the same client
  cross each other, it indicates a failure of internal firm-level netting
  logic. Both cases are flagged and `endtag` is retained in the output so
  the risk manager knows exactly which algorithms to investigate.

### Method
1. Restrict to TRADE events only — wash trades require actual executions.
2. For each (`client_tag`, `sym`) pair, extract all buy and sell events.
3. Use a **1-second rolling window on `ts_seconds`** to pair opposing sides.
4. Flag any case where a buy and sell from the same client on the same
   instrument occur within 1 second of each other.
5. Output paired receipts — buy and sell rows displayed side by side —
   so each flag is immediately verifiable.

### Trapdoor Fix — Time String Brittleness
Rather than grouping on the raw `tradets` string (which has only 100ms
precision and can create false positives from coincidental timestamp
sharing), I use integer `ts_seconds` as the grouping key. This enforces
a clean 1-second window that catches traders spacing their crosses by
milliseconds while avoiding false positives from normal sequential execution.

### Threshold Justification
A **1-second window** is the standard first-pass wash trade screen used
in algorithmic surveillance. It is tight enough to flag genuine simultaneous
crossing and wide enough to account for execution latency between legs.
Any flags produced are treated as candidates for compliance review, not
definitive proof of manipulation.

### Assumption
`client_tag` values are assumed to represent unique beneficial owners.
If multiple client tags map to the same underlying entity via prime
brokerage or give-up arrangements, true wash trading exposure would be
higher than reported here.


## Volume Spikes

### Definition
A volume spike is a minute where total traded quantity for a given
instrument is statistically far above that instrument's session average —
indicating an unusual burst that could represent a news event, algorithmic
malfunction, or deliberate market impact.

### Why This Matters
NAS100 block trades identified in Section 2.5 (max single trade $26.85M)
make this instrument the primary candidate for volume spike investigation.
If large NAS100 trades cluster in specific minutes, it suggests coordinated
block execution rather than organic flow.

### Method
1. Bucket all TRADE events into 1-minute windows per instrument.
2. Compute total quantity traded per instrument per minute.
3. Calculate Z-score: `(minute_qty − mean) / std` per instrument.
4. Flag any minute where Z-score > 2.
5. Cross-reference flagged minutes against the Section 2.5 whale trade
   table to identify whether block trades are driving the spikes.

### Trapdoor Fix — Fat Tails
Financial volume is rarely normally distributed — it is right-skewed with
fat tails, meaning a standard Z-score may produce more false positives than
the theoretical 2.5%. I treat the Z-score here as a **ranking heuristic**
rather than a strict statistical probability. A Z > 2 flag means "this
minute is in the extreme tail of the distribution and warrants review" —
not "this minute has a 2.5% probability of occurring by chance."
This distinction is stated explicitly so the threshold is not misapplied.

### Assumption
Minute-level volume is assumed to be approximately normally distributed
within each instrument as a first-pass approximation. Given the consistent
33% trade share observed across all instruments in Section 2.2, this
assumption is reasonable for this dataset.


## Cancel Surge

### Definition
A cancel surge is a minute where the ratio of CANCEL to TRADE events
spikes significantly above the session average — a potential signal of
spoofing, adverse selection, or liquidity stress.

### Why This Matters
Minute 39 was flagged in Section 2.4 as the session peak cancel-to-trade
ratio (0.76x). Section 3 drills into this minute to answer:
- Which instrument was driving the cancellations?
- Which strategy (`endtag`) was responsible?
- Was the spike isolated or part of a broader pattern?

### Method
1. Compute cancel-to-trade ratio per minute (reusing `time_stats` from 2.4).
2. Flag minutes where ratio > mean + 2σ.
3. Additionally flag minutes where raw CANCEL count exceeds the 95th
   percentile AND TRADE count is zero — catching pure cancel storms that
   the ratio alone would produce a division-by-zero for.
4. Drill down flagged minutes by instrument and `endtag` to identify the
   source of abnormal activity.

### Trapdoor Fix — Division by Zero
If TRADE = 0 in a given minute, cancel-to-trade ratio is undefined.
Rather than dropping these minutes (which would hide the most extreme
case — a minute with cancellations but zero fills), I handle them
separately using a raw count heuristic:

Flag if: CANCEL > 95th percentile of session cancel counts AND TRADE = 0

This ensures pure cancel storms are captured even when the ratio
formula cannot be applied.

### Threshold Justification
**Mean + 2σ** identifies the top ~2.5% of minutes by cancel intensity,
consistent with the volume spike threshold for cross-signal comparability.
The session cancel-to-trade ratio never crosses 1.0 (Section 2.4), so no
minute represents true liquidity stress — but relative spikes still warrant
investigation as potential precursors to adverse selection.

### Assumption
Cancel-to-trade ratio is computed at session level across all instruments.
A per-instrument breakdown is provided in the drill-down to prevent the
aggregate masking instrument-specific stress.


## Strategy Dominance

### Definition
A strategy dominance flag is raised when a single `endtag` accounts for
more than 50% of total traded volume in a specific instrument in any given
minute — indicating activity driven by a single algorithm rather than
organic multi-participant flow.

### Why This Matters
In a genuinely liquid market, volume should be distributed across multiple
participants. When one strategy dominates a minute:
- **Liquidity illusion:** Apparent activity is not real market depth.
- **Rogue algorithm risk:** A strategy consuming >50% of a minute's flow
  may be operating outside its intended parameters.

### Method
1. For each (minute, instrument) pair, calculate volume per `endtag`.
2. Express each endtag's volume as a percentage of the minute total.
3. Apply a **liquidity gate**: only raise a dominance flag if total
   volume for that (minute, instrument) exceeds the session's 75th
   percentile volume for that instrument.
4. Flag any (minute, endtag, instrument) combination where share > 50%
   AND the liquidity gate is satisfied.
5. Rank flagged minutes by dominance percentage.

### Trapdoor Fix — Low Volume Denominator
Without a liquidity gate, a minute where only 2 trades occur would
trivially flag the larger one as "dominant" — a meaningless result.
The **75th percentile liquidity gate** ensures dominance flags only
fire in minutes where there is meaningful activity to dominate.
Quiet minutes are ignored entirely.

### Threshold Justification
**50%** is the standard threshold for market dominance in regulatory
surveillance — the point at which a single participant controls the
majority of activity in a given window.

### Assumption
Dominance is measured in raw quantity units for consistency with the
volume spike analysis. A notional-based dominance measure would produce
different results for NAS100 vs FX pairs given the price level
differences identified in Section 2.5.


## Unified Anomaly Matrix

### Definition
A single cross-signal risk summary that compiles every `endtag` flagged
by any of the four signals and shows which signals it triggered.

### Why This Matters
A real risk manager does not review four separate reports. They need to
know: **"Is this strategy just noisy, or is it systematically broken?"**

The matrix answers this by showing signal co-occurrence:

| Co-occurrence Pattern | Interpretation |
|---|---|
| Volume Spike only | Large but potentially legitimate block trade |
| Cancel Surge only | Possible spoofing or adverse selection response |
| Volume Spike + Cancel Surge | Systemic malfunction — strategy is both active and withdrawing |
| Wash Trade + Dominance | Deliberate market manipulation pattern |
| All four signals | Highest priority — immediate compliance escalation |

### Method
1. Collect all flagged `endtag` values from each of the four signals.
2. Build a summary DataFrame with one row per `endtag`.
3. Mark each signal column as True/False per strategy.
4. Add a total flag count column for prioritisation.
5. Sort by total flags descending — highest risk strategies at the top.

### Output Format

endtag | wash_trade | vol_spike | cancel_surge | dominance | total_flags
